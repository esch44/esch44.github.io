Dual AGN with the Varstrometry Method

Dual and binary AGN are crucial to the study of the formation and evolution of supermassive black holes (SMBHs) and active galactic nuclei (AGN). It is well understood that major galaxy mergers result in pairs of gravitationally-bound SMBHs, some of which light up as AGN. However, these systems are notoriously difficult to unambiguously detect.

The Varstrometry Method

There are many methods proposed for the detection of dual AGN systems. One is known as the varstrometry (variability + astrometry) method, and combines high-precision astrometric measurements with high-resolution radio interferometry (radio+varstrometry; VaDAR). The space observatory Gaia has revealed a new population of quasars with high astrometric variance (or positional jitter on the sky), and it is hoped that this sample will reveal a new population of dual AGN.

My work combines observations from the Very Large Array with the high-precision astrometric measurements from Gaia. Additional follow-up observations include Very Long Baseline Interferometry (observations taken) and the Hubble Space Telescope (proposal submitted). A paper reporting the initial VLA results is in preparation.

Science Question

Can the varstrometry method, in combination with radio observations, reveal a new population of dual AGN? What does the radio+varstrometry method select for? What do the host galaxies of these quasars look like? Can this method be used to find dual AGN at higher redshifts and closer separations?
