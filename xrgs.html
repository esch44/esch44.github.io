X-shaped Radio Galaxies

X-shaped radio galaxies are fascinating, unique objects that are identified as hosting two separate sets of radio jets/lobes with different angular orientations, creating an 'X' shape, though they are sometimes seen as 'Z' or 'S' shapes. There are several possible physical drivers of XRGs, including (i) a restarted single SMBH producing jets with different orientations, (ii) jet deflection by the host galaxy's ISM (interstellar medium), and (iii) a binary AGN, via either reorientation due to coalescence or each SMBH producing a piar of jets.

The XRGs are not only interesting for their various possible physical drivers. Multi-AGN (dual and binary AGN) are one of the few physical explanations for this small but significant population. XRGs may be one interesting way to search specifically for the small separation binaries that have so far eluded us. There is only one confirmed binary AGN, with a separation of 7 kiloparsecs, which was confirmed with high resolution radio interferometry.

Science Question

What is the dominant physical driver of XRGs? Are there multiple drivers, and if so, are their physical conditions that determine which is dominant? Can a study of all known XRGs produce a new population of binary AGN? Do XRGs influence their surrounding environment, including the ISM and IGM of their host galaxies and galaxyc clusters? What can we learn from XRGs about the importance and impact of AGN feedback?
