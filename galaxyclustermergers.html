Galaxy Cluster Mergers

Galaxy clusters and their mergers are among the largest gravitationally bound systems in our universe. They assemble and evolve through accretino and mergers of less massive systems, and these mergers have enormous kinetic energy. They are crucial to studying physics on a cluster scale, with multiwavelength observations, in addition to physics on the much smaller scale of dark matter.

Cluster Mergers with Radio Observations

Comparative studies of X-ray and radio emission in merging clusters are required to fully understand the nature of non-thermal cluster components and diffuse synchrotron sources, such as relics, halos, and phoenixes. An additional open question is the mechanisms of relativistic electron production in astrophysical plasma, from which the diffuse synchrotron sources are generated.

My work focuses on the radio observations of a dissociative, post-core passage cluster merger known as CIZA J0107.7+5408. This is a remarkable example of a binary cluster merger hosting a rare combination of both a classical halo and possibly relic emission, in addition to two possible radio phoenixes that are bright at low frequencies. My focus is on radio observations from the Very Large Array (VLA) at S- and P-bands, in all four configurations. Additional observations include 74 MHz observations from the VLA's low-band system into order to characterize the morphology of the two possible ultra-steep spectrum sources.

Science Question

What is the morphology of this dissociative cluster merger? What structure and activity does the low-frequency study reveal? Can we complete a spectral index map, and identify any particularly steep spectrum areas of the merger? Can we confirm that the cluster constrain both a classical radio halo and relic emission, in addition to two ultra-steep spectrum phoenixes?
